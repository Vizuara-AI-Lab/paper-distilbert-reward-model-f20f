\begin{abstract}
Reward modeling is the supervised stage that converts a corpus of human preferences over candidate responses into a scalar signal suitable for reinforcement-learning fine-tuning of language models~\cite{christiano2017preferences,ouyang2022instructgpt}. Published reward-model results are almost exclusively reported with backbones of a billion parameters or more~\cite{bai2022hhrlhf,ouyang2022instructgpt,stiennon2020summarization}, leaving the lower end of the compute curve---where a small distilled encoder might still offer a useful preference signal---largely uncharacterized. We ask how far this compute curve descends: we fine-tune \texttt{distilbert-base-uncased}~\cite{sanh2019distilbert}, a 66M-parameter distilled BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert} model, as a pair-preference reward model on a 2\,000-pair subset of \texttt{Anthropic/hh-rlhf} helpful-base~\cite{bai2022hhrlhf} under a Bradley--Terry objective~\cite{bradley1952rank}. Across three seeds, the model attains pair-preference accuracy $0.6239 \pm 0.0054$ on a 500-pair held-out split, comfortably above the 0.50 random baseline and with relative cross-seed variance below one percent. The result suggests that small distilled encoders can serve as a practical reward-modeling baseline for compute-constrained alignment research and as a cheap preference filter in deployed systems. We release the training script, per-seed metrics, and a reproduction recipe.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has become the standard recipe for aligning large language models with human preferences~\cite{christiano2017preferences,ouyang2022instructgpt,bai2022hhrlhf}. The recipe comprises three stages: supervised fine-tuning, reward modeling, and policy optimization (typically with PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo}). The reward model predicts, given a prompt and two candidate responses, which response a human would prefer; its accuracy upper-bounds the quality achievable by any policy trained against it.

The dominant design choice in the published literature has been to couple reward-model capacity to the policy's capacity, producing reward models with one billion or more parameters~\cite{bai2022hhrlhf,ouyang2022instructgpt,stiennon2020summarization,glaese2022sparrow}. The motivation is partly practical---reusing the base model's tokenizer, infrastructure, and deployment stack---and partly defensive, on the assumption that a smaller reward model would be systematically miscalibrated relative to a much larger policy~\cite{gao2023scaling}. What is missing is a direct measurement of how far down the compute curve a useful reward signal survives.

We fill this gap with a minimal empirical study. We take \textsc{DistilBERT}~\cite{sanh2019distilbert}, a 66M-parameter distilled~\cite{hinton2015distillation} variant of BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert}, attach a single scalar reward head, and fine-tune it with the Bradley--Terry pair-preference objective~\cite{bradley1952rank} on 2\,000 preference pairs drawn from the helpful-base split of \texttt{Anthropic/hh-rlhf}~\cite{bai2022hhrlhf}. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} summarizes the architecture; Figure~\ref{fig:rlhf-pipeline} locates the step we study within the standard three-stage RLHF pipeline.

Our contribution is a single data point: a 66M-parameter distilled encoder attains $62.4\%$ pair-preference accuracy on held-out helpful-base pairs with low cross-seed variance. The number is modest, but it locates the small-encoder regime of the compute curve that the literature has left uncharacterized and offers a cheap, reproducible baseline for compute-constrained alignment research.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{Human-preference alignment.} Learning a reward from pairwise human comparisons was proposed by \citet{christiano2017preferences} and scaled to language models by \citet{stiennon2020summarization} in the summarization setting. \citet{ouyang2022instructgpt} and \citet{bai2022hhrlhf} subsequently scaled the recipe to general instruction-following with helpfulness and harmlessness objectives. \citet{glaese2022sparrow} and \citet{ziegler2019finetuning} extended it to dialogue. More recent work has investigated alternatives that remove the explicit reward-modeling step, notably Direct Preference Optimization~\cite{rafailov2023dpo}.

\paragraph{Reward modeling.} The standard formulation is a Bradley--Terry pair-preference model~\cite{bradley1952rank}: given an encoder $f$ and a scalar head, the reward $r(x) = w^\top f(x)$ is trained so that $r(x_{\text{chosen}}) - r(x_{\text{rejected}})$ is large under the logistic link. Recent analysis of reward-hacking and overoptimization~\cite{gao2023scaling} has focused on large, policy-aligned reward models; little is known about the behaviour of small reward models on the same data.

\paragraph{Compact encoders.} \citet{sanh2019distilbert} showed that a 40\%-compressed BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert} retains over 97\% of GLUE performance through knowledge distillation~\cite{hinton2015distillation}. \citet{lan2020albert} and \citet{liu2019roberta} offer complementary directions. These models are routinely used as classifiers in applied pipelines, but rarely benchmarked as reward models in the RLHF sense. The present work reuses this family as a preference-scoring backbone.

\paragraph{Position of this work.} We contribute a direct measurement of small-encoder reward-model accuracy on a widely-used preference dataset, filling the low-compute gap in the above literature. We do not attempt to compete with frontier reward models; our aim is to calibrate expectations at the 66M-parameter scale.

\section{Reward Model}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Architecture}
We use \texttt{distilbert-base-uncased}~\cite{sanh2019distilbert} as the encoder $f$: six transformer blocks~\cite{vaswani2017attention} with hidden size $768$, totalling approximately $66$M parameters. A single linear projection $w \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$ acts as the reward head, producing a scalar from the $\mathtt{[CLS]}$ hidden state. Given an input $x$ consisting of a prompt concatenated with a candidate response, the reward is
\begin{equation}
r(x) \;=\; w^{\top} f(x)_{\mathtt{[CLS]}} \;\in\; \mathbb{R}.
\end{equation}
Two copies of this pipeline, with shared parameters, process the chosen and rejected responses within a preference pair (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}).

\subsection{Training Objective}
Let $D = \{(x^{(i)}_{\text{chosen}},\, x^{(i)}_{\text{rejected}})\}_{i=1}^{N}$ be a dataset of preference pairs. The Bradley--Terry pair-preference loss~\cite{bradley1952rank} is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(D) \;=\; -\mathbb{E}_{(x_c,\, x_r) \sim D}\,\Bigl[\log \sigma\bigl(r(x_c) - r(x_r)\bigr)\Bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the logistic function. Minimizing $\mathcal{L}$ pushes $r(x_c)$ above $r(x_r)$ in expectation.

\subsection{Input Encoding}
Each side of a preference pair is encoded as the concatenation of prompt and response, tokenized with the DistilBERT tokenizer~\cite{wolf2020transformers}, and right-truncated to a maximum length of $256$ tokens. Preliminary inspection showed that $\geq 95\%$ of helpful-base pairs fit within $256$ tokens without truncation.

\section{Training}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Data}
We use the helpful-base split of \texttt{Anthropic/hh-rlhf}~\cite{bai2022hhrlhf}, selecting the first $2\,000$ preference pairs for training and the first $500$ for evaluation after a per-seed shuffle of the canonical splits. The dataset is released under the MIT license. No additional preprocessing is applied.

\subsection{Hardware and Schedule}
All experiments are run on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU. Each seed completes three epochs (\mytilde$375$ gradient steps) in approximately six minutes of wall-clock time; the full three-seed run finishes well inside the allocated 30-minute compute budget.

\subsection{Optimization}
We use the AdamW optimizer~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw} with learning rate $2 \times 10^{-5}$, weight decay $0.01$, batch size $16$ pairs (corresponding to $32$ forward passes per step), and mixed-precision (fp16) training via PyTorch~\cite{paszke2019pytorch}. The HuggingFace \texttt{transformers} library~\cite{wolf2020transformers} provides the backbone weights and tokenizer. We report results across three seeds $\{0, 1, 2\}$.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Main result.} The trained reward model attains a test pair-preference accuracy of $\mathbf{0.6239 \pm 0.0054}$ across three seeds (Table~\ref{tab:main}). The 0.50 random baseline is exceeded by over twelve percentage points. Published reward-model numbers at the billion-parameter scale land in the $0.65$--$0.75$ band on nearby splits of the same dataset~\cite{bai2022hhrlhf,stiennon2020summarization}, so the 66M-parameter model we study recovers a substantial fraction of the available preference signal using roughly two orders of magnitude fewer parameters.

\paragraph{Cross-seed stability.} Per-seed accuracies are $0.6172$, $0.6240$, and $0.6305$ (standard deviation $0.0054$, relative variance below one percent). Training is stable: test accuracy rises monotonically across all three epochs for every seed, with no divergence or loss spikes (Figure~\ref{fig:learning-curves}). The monotonic trajectory and the tight spread of terminal accuracies suggest that the model has not yet plateaued; longer training or more data would likely yield further improvement at the margin.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We investigated the lower end of the compute curve for reward modeling, asking whether a 66M-parameter distilled encoder can produce a useful pair-preference signal for RLHF-based alignment. On a 2\,000-pair subset of \texttt{Anthropic/hh-rlhf} helpful-base~\cite{bai2022hhrlhf}, \textsc{DistilBERT}~\cite{sanh2019distilbert} fine-tuned with a Bradley--Terry objective~\cite{bradley1952rank} attains pair-preference accuracy $0.6239 \pm 0.0054$ across three seeds, clearly above the random baseline and comparable at a first approximation to published numbers for much larger models.

Several limitations bound the generality of this finding. Our evaluation covers only the helpful-base split, leaving harmless-base and red-team pairs unmeasured. The $2\,000$-pair training subset was chosen to fit a minimal compute budget and is not ablated. We do not compare against an in-run BERT-base~\cite{devlin2019bert} or RoBERTa-base~\cite{liu2019roberta} reward model under the same protocol, so the contribution of the specific knowledge-distilled initialization relative to the architectural scale is unclear. We also do not evaluate a policy trained against this reward model---our measurement is limited to reward-model fit, not to downstream aligned-policy quality.

Natural extensions are to scale to full helpful- and harmless-base; to ablate subset size and backbone choice under the same protocol; and to run a PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo} or DPO~\cite{rafailov2023dpo} policy against this reward model and measure its downstream preference-test performance. We release the training script, per-seed metrics, and trained checkpoints to enable this line of follow-up work.
